# Project Rules

## Commit Convention
- For feature commits, use an English subject line with `feat(<project-scope>): ...`, and set `<project-scope>` to the current project/module name.
- Use Chinese in the commit body, formatted as numbered items `1. 2. 3.`.
- Before committing, check whether runtime/state files should be added to `.gitignore`.

## Content Writing Rules
- Treat public writing as sharing an object, a method, or a result. Do not treat it as a place to ask readers to watch your taste, status, or self-image.
- Prefer writing from one of these entry points: `I discovered something`, `I built something`, `I was genuinely struck by something`.
- Let readers infer who you are from stable output over time. Do not rush to define yourself in the text.
- Put the subject in front and the self behind. If removing the self-display layer weakens nothing, the draft is usually safe.
- Publish only when the content has clear external value: a concrete observation, a tested method, a finished result, a useful warning, or a well-scoped question.
- Keep half-formed reflections, travel feelings, and identity work in private notes unless they have been rewritten into a public problem with a public entry point.
- For travel writing, do not force a grand thesis. Record how the trip changed or corrected an earlier understanding rather than pretending the trip solved a large historical question.
- For public technical posts, show the reasoning process, the key judgment, and the reusable method. Avoid writing to prove that you are advanced or special.

## Reverse Engineering Notes
- Record reverse engineering work as analysis notes and method writeups, not as conquest reports.
- Prefer `phenomenon -> hypothesis -> validation -> model -> reusable method`.
- Showcase one key point when possible. A single sharp technical judgment is better than a long victory post.
- Emphasize how a conclusion was reached: what was observed, why a hypothesis formed, how it was verified, and what was learned for future cases.
- Do not publish target details, operationally sensitive business logic, turnkey bypass steps, or material that is easy to misuse.
- Stop the public writeup once the method or model is established. Do not continue into full target teardown just to make the post look stronger.

## Public Content Layers
- `Published posts`: use for content with durable external value, especially methods, tooling, tested experience, and restrained technical notes.
- `Public archive notes`: use for experiments, partial analyses, reading traces, travel fragments, and material worth keeping public but not worth promoting as a finished piece.
- `Outline-only notes`: keep a title, bullets, or a short question when the idea is real but not ready for a full post.
- `Social posts`: keep them lightweight. Share something genuinely interesting, useful, or funny instead of broadcasting a self-image.

## Draft Self-Check
- Does this draft share a thing, or ask readers to watch me?
- If nobody reacted, would I still believe the note was worth writing?
- Is the core value a concrete observation, method, result, or question?
- Have I kept "I am special / sharp / tasteful" out of the implied message?
- For technical notes, did I focus on process and reusable method instead of sensitive details or victory framing?
- For travel or reflective notes, did I avoid pretending to solve a big question and instead record what became more concrete?
- After removing the self-display layer, does the piece still stand?
